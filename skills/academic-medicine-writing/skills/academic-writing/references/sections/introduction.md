# Introduction

## Core Chain

Use this chain for medical introductions:

```text
problem -> evidence gap -> objective
```

The full version is:

```text
clinical/public-health/biomedical problem -> what is known -> what remains uncertain or biased ->
why this study design can answer the gap -> objective -> bounded contribution
```

## Paragraph Plan

| Paragraph | Role | Required content |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | problem and setting | specific clinical, public-health, or biomedical problem; avoid generic burden unless needed |
| 2 | current evidence | what prior work, guidelines, or practice already establish |
| 3 | gap | the unresolved evidence gap, uncertainty, bias, population gap, method gap, or endpoint gap |
| 4 | objective | exact study objective, design, population, and primary outcome |

Use fewer paragraphs for short formats. Use more only when the target article type and evidence
justify it.

## Evidence Rules

- Cite disease burden, standard of care, guidelines, diagnostic thresholds, and prior systematic
  reviews when they are used to motivate the study.
- Do not cite every related study. Select sources that establish the problem, current knowledge,
  and gap.
- If citation support is missing during drafting, weaken the background and mark the citation gap
  rather than writing a confident claim.

## Objective Sentence

The final introduction sentence should match the Abstract objective and Methods design:

```text
We aimed to evaluate/estimate/compare/develop/validate <objective> among <population> in <setting>,
using <design/data source>, with <primary outcome> as the main outcome.
```

Use "assess association" for observational designs unless causal evidence is confirmed. Use
"develop and validate" only when both steps exist. Use "diagnostic accuracy" only when the index
test/reference standard structure exists.

## Do Not

- open with broad textbook disease description when the study question is specific,
- make the Introduction a mini Results section,
- imply clinical utility, treatment recommendation, or causal effect before the study design
  supports it,
- introduce endpoints or populations that Methods and Results do not carry.
