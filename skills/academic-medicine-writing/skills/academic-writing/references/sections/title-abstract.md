# Title, Abstract, And Key Points

## Writing Readiness

Do not draft the title, structured abstract, Key Points, or summary box until these inputs are known
or explicitly marked unknown:

- study design and article type,
- population, setting, and timeframe,
- intervention, exposure, index test, predictor, or comparison frame,
- primary objective and primary outcome,
- primary result with denominator, estimate, and uncertainty when available,
- main limitation or boundary that affects interpretation,
- target journal abstract structure and word limit when named.

## Title Logic

The title should state the study object without overstating inference strength.

| Study design | Safer title move |
|---|---|
| randomized trial | intervention, population, and trial design |
| observational cohort/case-control/cross-sectional | exposure/outcome association and design |
| diagnostic accuracy | index test, target condition, and study design |
| prediction model | model purpose, population, and development/validation status |
| systematic review/meta-analysis | intervention/exposure/test/topic and review type |
| case report | clinical phenomenon, not a general treatment claim |

Avoid causal verbs in observational titles unless the source evidence supports causal inference.
Avoid "clinical utility" unless patient-management impact was actually evaluated.
For JAMA/JAMA-family routes, do not include study type or design in the title or subtitle for
observational, diagnostic/prognostic, quality-improvement, survey, or other nontrial original data
reports. Only clinical trials and meta-analyses should carry a design subtitle such as "A Randomized
Clinical Trial" or "A Meta-analysis" unless the current official instructions say otherwise. Put the
study design in the abstract Design heading and Methods instead.

## Structured Abstract Order

Use target journal headings when available. For a generic medical article, use:

1. Importance/Background: one sentence for the problem and evidence gap.
2. Objective: one sentence with the exact objective.
3. Design, Setting, and Participants: design, source, dates, and eligible population.
4. Exposure/Intervention/Test/Predictors: only the relevant design component.
5. Main Outcomes and Measures: primary outcome and prespecified secondary outcomes when needed.
6. Results: sample size, primary result, uncertainty, key secondary/safety result if supported.
7. Conclusions and Relevance: bounded interpretation, not clinical advice.

## Result Sentence Gate

An abstract result sentence must include the analysis denominator or sample size, the effect estimate
or performance metric, and uncertainty when source material provides it. If the estimate is missing,
state the direction or descriptive result only when that is supported.

## Key Points

For JAMA-style Key Points:

- Question: one sentence matching the objective.
- Findings: one sentence with design, sample, and primary result; focus on the primary outcome and
  avoid odds ratios, confidence intervals, p values, long provenance caveats, and secondary outcomes.
- Meaning: one sentence with a cautious implication inside the study boundary.
Keep the total Key Points block inside the active JAMA word budget. If the only defensible meaning is
"workflow-validation only" or "not real patient evidence", state that boundary once rather than
repeating it across all three items.

## Common Failures

- title claims treatment benefit from observational association,
- abstract conclusion introduces a recommendation absent from Discussion,
- abstract omits denominators or uncertainty while the source has them,
- Key Points contain broader claims than the manuscript body,
- abstract reports outcomes that Methods did not define.
