# Literature Positioning

Use this guide for Background, Related Work, prior-work paragraphs in Introduction, and comparison
paragraphs in Discussion. The writing job is not to list papers; it is to move from
current knowledge -> unresolved gap -> study contribution without inflating the study's evidence
strength.

## Writing Readiness

Before drafting, confirm:

- the clinical, public-health, biomedical, or translational problem being positioned;
- the exact population, disease, exposure/intervention/test/predictor, or biological system;
- the strongest verified sources for current knowledge;
- the unresolved gap that the present study can actually address;
- the study's design strength and limits;
- whether the target journal expects a brief Introduction or a more explicit prior-work synthesis.

If citation support is thin, write narrower background prose and mark the citation gap. Do not
invent disease burden, guideline positions, standard-of-care claims, or prior-study results.

## Paragraph Pattern

Use a three-move pattern:

1. Current knowledge: one sentence that states the accepted fact or prior evidence, with verified
   citation support.
2. Unresolved gap: one sentence that names the uncertainty, reporting gap, population gap, setting
   gap, method gap, or external-validity gap.
3. Study contribution: one sentence that explains what this study tests, estimates, describes, or
   develops, using design-appropriate verbs.

Keep the contribution sentence bounded by the study type. Observational studies estimate
associations or describe patterns; diagnostic studies evaluate test accuracy against a reference
standard; prediction studies develop or validate models; translational studies test biological
signals or mechanisms under specified conditions.

## Introduction Use

In Introduction, literature positioning should narrow quickly:

- Problem paragraph: why the question matters for the named population or biological context.
- Evidence paragraph: what prior work establishes and what remains unresolved.
- Objective paragraph: what this manuscript evaluates, with design, population/system, and endpoint.

Avoid a long generic disease-burden opening unless the burden claim directly motivates the study
question and has verified support.

## Discussion Use

In Discussion, compare the principal finding with prior work before giving implications:

- State whether the present result is consistent with, extends, or differs from prior evidence.
- Explain plausible reasons for differences using design, population, measurement, setting, or
  analysis differences.
- Separate external comparison from mechanism speculation.
- End with implications that match evidence strength and checklist status.

Do not use prior-work comparison to erase limitations. A finding can be consistent with prior
studies and still remain limited by bias, missingness, small sample size, internal validation only,
or incomplete mechanistic support.

## Common Failures

- Background claims are citation-free or rely on unverified secondary statements.
- A broad guideline or standard-of-care claim appears without an official or authoritative source.
- The gap is generic, such as "few studies exist," without naming population, endpoint, setting, or
  method.
- Discussion turns an association, exploratory signal, or internal validation result into clinical
  utility.
- Prior studies are summarized as a bibliography list rather than used to define the unresolved gap.
