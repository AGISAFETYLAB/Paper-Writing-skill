# Finance Prose Style And Section Formulas

Use this reference whenever drafting, revising, polishing, compressing, or auditing finance
manuscript prose. It adapts economist-writing conventions to finance papers without importing
investment-advice language.

## Reader-first finance writing

Write for busy financial economists, not for a generic business audience. Put the economic question,
data setting, method, main result, and magnitude early. Do not make the reader wait until the results
section to learn what the paper finds.

Core rules:

- State the result, not only the activity. Prefer "a one-standard-deviation increase in governance
  quality is associated with 4.2 percentage points lower leverage" over "we examine governance and
  leverage".
- Tie every empirical sentence to sample, unit, model, uncertainty, and magnitude when those details
  are material.
- Separate market implication from trading advice. "The return spread is not explained by the
  benchmark factors in this sample" is acceptable; "investors should trade this signal" is not.
- Use causal verbs only when the design supports them. Otherwise use "is associated with",
  "predicts", "is consistent with", or "suggests".

## Triangular structure

Use triangular structure in abstracts, introductions, results paragraphs, and conclusions:

1. Lead with the claim or result.
2. Give the data/model and identification or benchmark that supports it.
3. Interpret the economic magnitude.
4. Then add robustness, mechanisms, limitations, and literature positioning.

Do not begin with broad claims such as "Financial markets are important" or "Corporate governance
has received considerable attention". Start with the finance puzzle, institutional shock, return
pattern, model friction, or policy question.

## Anti-AI pattern ban

Delete or replace generic AI-sounding prose:

- banned fillers: "delve", "landscape", "multifaceted", "pivotal", "groundbreaking",
  "shed light on", "pave the way", "it is worth noting", "it should be noted"
- weak finance adjectives: "interesting", "important", "robust" without saying to what
- empty contribution phrases: "contributes to the literature by" when the actual result can be
  stated directly
- generic method phrases: "we utilize a comprehensive dataset" instead of naming the data source,
  sample window, unit, and variable construction

Use field terms naturally: alpha, factor model, portfolio sort, abnormal return, spread, treatment,
control, fixed effects, clustering, liquidity, leverage, default, disclosure, governance. Define
specialized terms the first time they appear.

## Title and abstract formula

Title:

- Name the finance object and economic relation: treatment/outcome, signal/return, friction/result,
  or policy/effect.
- Avoid leading with the method unless the method is the contribution.
- For asset-pricing papers, name the anomaly, factor, signal, or mechanism.
- For theory papers, name the friction or mechanism, not the algebra.

Abstract Formula:

1. What question the paper answers.
2. What data/model/design answers it.
3. What the paper finds, with magnitude.
4. Why the finding changes beliefs for finance or financial economics.

Keep the abstract concrete. Do not cite literature unless a short puzzle citation is essential.
For journal targets with stricter abstract limits, obey the venue card or current official
instructions.

## Introduction formula

Use this default structure unless a paper-type card overrides it:

1. Hook: finance puzzle, institutional fact, market event, model tension, or policy question.
2. Research question and contribution: one paragraph that says what the paper does and finds.
3. Data/model/design: source of variation, sample, unit, method, benchmark, or model friction.
4. Main results: concrete estimates, magnitudes, and uncertainty.
5. Mechanisms and robustness: only the tests that change reader beliefs.
6. Literature positioning: story-driven, closest papers only, with a clear "what remains open".
7. Roadmap: short and specific.

The main result should appear in the first three paragraphs. Literature review belongs inside the
introduction unless the target journal or paper type clearly requires a separate related-work
section.

## Literature Review As Story

Do not write an annotated bibliography. Group the closest papers by what they establish and what
they leave unresolved:

- what the field already knows
- where the identification, data, model, or setting remains incomplete
- why this paper resolves that gap or narrows the uncertainty

Use full author names in prose where clarity matters. Prefer published versions over working-paper
versions unless the working paper is the current authoritative version.

## Results Presentation

Start each results subsection with the economic conclusion, then give the table or figure evidence.
For each central result:

- report magnitude in economic units
- separate statistical significance from economic significance
- say what the benchmark, comparison group, or omitted category is
- state standard-error clustering or dependence treatment
- explain robustness in terms of the threat it addresses

Null results are results only when confidence intervals or power make the absence of an economically
meaningful effect interpretable.

## Theory And Model Section

For finance theory papers:

1. Start from the economic friction or puzzle.
2. Describe agents, timing, information, constraints, and payoff objects in words before equations.
3. State assumptions and identify which are essential versus simplifying.
4. Present propositions with intuition before proofs.
5. Explain comparative statics in finance language.
6. Put long proofs in the appendix unless they carry the main mechanism.

For mixed theory-empirical papers, map each empirical test to a proposition or prediction.

## Conclusion formula

Use three parts:

1. Restate the main result in different language from the abstract.
2. Explain implication for finance theory, market behavior, firms, intermediaries, or policy.
3. Name one or two concrete limits or future tests.

Do not introduce new evidence. Do not end with generic "future research is needed" language.
